¬∂Cohesion on the Moon. The iconic photo of Buzz Aldrin‚Äôs boot print on the moon (below) came about because of a little-known, but deliberate, experiment‚Äîone to help characterize the physical properties, including the extremely high cohesion factor, of lunar regolith (Apollo 11 soil mechanics test report). While we‚Äôve documented many additional characteristics of regolith since (see, for example, the NASA Engineering Guide to Lunar Geotechnical Properties), we don‚Äôt completely understand why it exhibits such high cohesion, especially in low lunar gravity. Understanding the Moon‚Äôs surface environment and the effects of its abrasive and cohesive regolith are important both for future lunar robotic missions (regolith mining, lunar radio telescopes) and for humans to learn how to live and work on the Moon safely. Regolith samples, low-g experiments, and simulations all help better our understanding of lunar surface characteristics. An example of this from just two weeks ago: The Swedish Negative Ions at the Lunar Surface (NILS) payload detected negatively charged ions on the far side surface, formed as secondary particles from solar wind particles colliding with the surface. NILS is ESA‚Äôs first lunar surface instrument, hosted aboard Chang‚Äôe 6. The Moon‚Äôs surface charge changes between the light and dark sides, with the light side generally being positively charged while the dark side is negatively charged. Negative ions can even cause regolith dust to levitate a meter or more above the surface and could be one force contributing to regolith cohesion.   • The flagship NASA mission to Uranus proposed by the recent Decadal survey could serve a dual purpose by also detecting gravitational waves. If the mission broadcasts its position every few seconds for the duration of its cruise, aggregated and processed Doppler ranging measurements could be sensitive to gravitational waves in the range of a tenth of a hertz to a billionth of a hertz (paper).
  • The way that kidneys process salts is altered by space flight (via some combination of radiation exposure and microgravity). This is known to cause kidney stones, and new evidence suggests that it may also cause potentially severe kidney disease over Mars transit times (paper).

  • Sometime this year, the white dwarf star T Corona Borealis, located 800 light-years away, will probably release a nova, expected to be the brightest such explosion visible from Earth since 1975. It should be as bright as Polaris, visible in the northern crown constellation with the naked eye, even in light-polluted areas. A nova, distinct from a supernova, occurs when hydrogen from a companion star accretes and builds an atmosphere around a white dwarf until a critical threshold is passed and the gas undergoes fusion and explodes. T Corona Borealis repeats its cycle of accretion and explosion roughly every 80 years, which is why it‚Äôs also known as the Blaze star. Its last eruption was in 1946. ‚ÄúAstronomers currently predict about a 70% chance that T Corona Borealis will go nova by September, and a 95% chance that it‚Äôll go off by the end of the year.‚Äù
